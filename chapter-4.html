How to read this page

Chapter 4 of the thesis is split into four subchapters. This page mirrors that exact order. Section 4.1 introduces the EU-wide gender gap on three political dimensions; 4.2 zooms into the extremes; 4.3 compares welfare regimes; 4.4 examines individual-level predictors.

Every chart is a live interactive figure: hover to inspect values, click legend entries to toggle series, and use the mode bar (top-right of each chart) to zoom, pan, or download a PNG.

4.1 — The Gen Z gender gap in political orientation

EU-wide gendered political attitudes, compared across generations and year-of-birth cohorts on three CHES-linked dimensions: left-right ideology, postmaterialist-traditional values, and support for women's rights.

Respondents from the European Social Survey (fieldwork 2023-2024) and the European Election Study (fieldwork 2024) were linked to party-position estimates from the Chapel Hill Expert Survey (2024). Reported votes are therefore situated consistently on left-right, postmaterialist-traditional, and women's-rights dimensions. Two complementary views are offered below: aggregation by generation, and year-of-birth trends smoothed with centered five-year rolling windows. Pooled EU27 sample, n = 36,082.

4.2 — Generational differences at the political extremes

Shift of attention from central tendencies to the ideological tails (CHES ≤ 2.5 or ≥ 7.5): share of respondents at the progressive and traditional extremes across generations, and the Gender Divergence in Change (GDC) index for the Millennials-to-Gen-Z transition.

4.4 — Predictors of the gender gap

Gen Z individual-level predictors of the postmaterialist-traditional alignment: economic difficulty, education, place of residence, parental origin, religious attendance and denomination, employment status, occupational class.
